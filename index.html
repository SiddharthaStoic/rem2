Apparent Motion of the Sun

The apparent motion of the sun refers to the perceived daily movement of the sun across the sky as observed from a fixed point on Earth. This motion is primarily due to the Earth's rotation on its axis and its orbit around the sun.

  1. Daily Path of the Sun: As Earth rotates from west to east, the sun appears to rise in the east, travel across the sky, and set in the west. This daily path varies in altitude and azimuth depending on the observer's latitude, the time of year, and the position of the Earth in its orbit around the sun.
  1. Sun’s Position Throughout the Day:
    • Morning: The sun rises in the eastern part of the sky. During this time, the shadows are long and cast towards the west.
    • Noon: The sun reaches its highest point in the sky, known as solar noon. At this time, shadows are shortest and point directly north or south, depending on the hemisphere.
    • Afternoon to Sunset: After reaching its zenith, the sun starts descending towards the western horizon, causing shadows to lengthen towards the east.
  1. Seasonal Variation: The sun's apparent path changes with the seasons due to the tilt of the Earth's axis (about 23.5 degrees relative to its orbital plane).
    • Summer Solstice: The sun follows its highest path across the sky, resulting in longer days and shorter shadows at noon.
    • Winter Solstice: The sun follows a lower path, resulting in shorter days and longer shadows.
    • Equinoxes: The sun rises exactly in the east and sets exactly in the west. The day and night are approximately equal in length.

Solar Thermal Systems

Solar collector is a device for collecting solar radiation. It also transfers the energy to a fluid passing in contact with it

Non-Concentrating Solar Collectors

Non-concentrating solar collectors, also known as flat-plate collectors, are devices designed to absorb sunlight over a large area without concentrating it onto a specific focal point.

Instead of using mirrors or lenses to focus sunlight, these collectors rely on a flat, glazed plate to capture solar radiation directly.

Flat Plate Collector

  1. Absorption of Solar Radiation: The flat-plate collector is composed of several layers. The top layer is typically made of a transparent material, such as glass or plastic, which allows sunlight to pass through while minimizing heat loss. Beneath the transparent cover is the absorber plate, which is usually made of a dark-colored material with high thermal conductivity, such as metal. The absorber plate absorbs sunlight and converts it into heat.
  1. Heat Transfer to Fluid: A series of tubes or channels are attached to the absorber plate. A heat transfer fluid, such as water or antifreeze, flows through these tubes or channels. As the absorber plate absorbs sunlight, it heats up, transferring heat to the fluid flowing through the tubes.
  1. Insulation: Behind the absorber plate is insulation material, which helps to minimize heat loss from the back of the collector. This insulation layer improves the collector's efficiency by reducing heat loss to the surroundings.
  1. Circulation: A pump circulates the heated fluid from the collector to a storage tank or heat exchanger. In a closed-loop system, the heated fluid can be used directly for space heating, water heating, or other applications. In an open-loop system, the heated fluid may pass through a heat exchanger to transfer its heat to a separate water supply.
  1. Control Systems: Control systems, such as temperature sensors and thermostatic valves, may be used to regulate the flow of fluid through the collector and maintain optimal operating temperatures.

Evacuated Tubular Collector

  1. Tube Design: Each tube in an evacuated tube collector is made of high-quality borosilicate glass, which is highly transparent and has excellent thermal insulation properties. The tube is sealed at both ends to create a vacuum inside, hence the name "evacuated tube." The vacuum minimizes heat loss through convection and conduction, improving the collector's efficiency.
  1. Absorber Coating: Inside each tube is a metal absorber coating, usually made of copper or aluminum, which absorbs sunlight and converts it into heat. The absorber coating is designed to have high solar absorptance and low thermal emissivity to maximize energy absorption and minimize heat loss.
  1. Heat Transfer Fluid: A heat transfer fluid, such as water or a mixture of water and antifreeze, flows through the absorber tubes to collect the heat absorbed from sunlight. The fluid absorbs thermal energy from the absorber coating and carries it away from the collector for use in heating applications.
  1. Manifold Assembly: The evacuated tubes are connected to a manifold assembly, which distributes the heat transfer fluid to and from the tubes. The manifold also provides structural support for the collector and facilitates the connection to the heating system.
  1. Circulation: A pump circulates the heat transfer fluid between the collector and a storage tank or heat exchanger. In a closed-loop system, the heated fluid is used directly for space heating, water heating, or other applications.

Concentrating Solar Collectors

Concentrating collectors are a type of solar collector that uses mirrors or lenses to concentrate sunlight onto a small area, thereby increasing the intensity of the solar energy captured.

These collectors are designed to focus sunlight onto a specific point or line, rather than simply absorbing sunlight over a large area like non-concentrating collectors.

Line Focusing Concentrating Collector

  • A line focusing concentrating collector is a type of solar energy technology that concentrates sunlight onto a linear receiver using reflective surfaces such as mirrors or lenses.
  • These collectors are designed to increase the intensity of sunlight on the receiver, which typically contains a fluid that absorbs the solar energy and transfers it for various applications like electricity generation or thermal heating.
  • This concentration of sunlight allows for more efficient conversion of solar energy into usable forms, making it a valuable tool in renewable energy systems.

There are three types:

Parabolic Trough Collector: This is one of the most established concentrating solar power (CSP) technologies. It consists of a long, curved (parabolic) mirror that focuses sunlight onto a receiver tube running along the focal line of the parabola. The receiver contains a heat transfer fluid (such as oil) that absorbs the concentrated sunlight's heat, which is then used to generate steam to drive a turbine and produce electricity.

Linear Fresnel Reflector: Similar to the parabolic trough collector, but instead of using a single curved mirror, it uses an array of flat mirrors (Fresnel reflectors) to concentrate sunlight onto a linear receiver tube. The mirrors are arranged in a way that they can individually adjust to track the sun's movement and maintain concentration on the receiver. This technology offers potential cost savings compared to parabolic troughs due to simpler construction.

Compound Parabolic Concentrator (CPC): A compound parabolic concentrator is a non-imaging optical device used to efficiently collect and focus light onto a receiver. It consists of two or more parabolic sections joined together, which allows it to capture light rays over a wide range of angles and direct them onto a small area, achieving high concentration ratios. CPCs are often used in solar water heaters, photovoltaic systems, and other solar energy applications.

Point Focusing Concentrating Collector

These collectors capture the sun's energy with mirrors that reflect and focus the sunlight into a receiver or absorber, and create the heat which is used to generate electricity.

A heat transfer medium is used to generate steam at 400°C (750°F) which is used to drive a steam turbine generator.

Parabolic Dish Collector: A parabolic dish reflector (PDR) is a point-focus system with a paraboloid geometry given by the revolution of one half of a parabola around its normal axis.

Sunlight entering the collector aperture with a normal incidence is concentrated onto a heat receiver located at the focal point of the dish.

Solar Power Tower

A solar power tower, also known as a solar tower or solar thermal power tower, is a type of concentrating solar power (CSP) system that uses a field of mirrors, called heliostats, to concentrate sunlight onto a central receiver mounted atop a tall tower.

This technology harnesses solar energy to produce electricity through the conversion of sunlight into high-temperature heat.

Heliostat Field: An array of flat mirrors, called heliostats, is spread across a large area surrounding the central tower. These heliostats track the movement of the sun throughout the day and reflect sunlight onto a single point at the top of the tower.

Central Receiver: At the top of the tower sits a receiver, which absorbs the concentrated sunlight. The receiver contains a heat transfer fluid, such as molten salt or liquid sodium, which is heated to extremely high temperatures (often exceeding 500°C or 932°F) by the concentrated sunlight.

Heat Transfer and Energy Conversion: The heated fluid from the receiver is then circulated down the tower to a heat exchanger, where it transfers its thermal energy to a working fluid (often water or steam), which then drives a turbine generator to produce electricity.

Power Generation: The turbine generator converts the thermal energy into electricity, which can be fed into the grid for distribution to consumers.

Solar Air Collector

  • A solar air collector is a type of solar thermal collector that harnesses sunlight to heat air.
  • Unlike solar photovoltaic (PV) panels, which generate electricity from sunlight, solar air collectors directly convert solar energy into heat, making them useful for space heating, ventilation, and drying applications.
  • A solar air collector typically consists of a dark-colored, heat-absorbing surface (such as a metal or polymer absorber plate) housed in an insulated enclosure with a transparent cover (such as glass or plastic). The transparent cover allows sunlight to enter while trapping heat inside the collector.
  • Sunlight passes through the transparent cover and strikes the absorber plate. The dark color of the absorber plate helps it absorb a large portion of the sunlight, converting it into heat.
  • Air from the surrounding environment or a designated space is drawn into the collector through inlet vents. As the air passes over the heated absorber plate, it absorbs heat from the plate's surface, increasing in temperature.
  • Solar air collectors can be installed on rooftops, walls, or as standalone units in locations with ample sunlight. They are particularly useful in off-grid or remote areas where access to conventional heating fuels may be limited or expensive.

Solar Distillation

  • Solar distillation is a method of water purification or desalination that utilizes solar energy to evaporate water and collect the resulting condensate.
  • It's particularly useful in regions where access to clean water is limited, such as arid areas or coastal regions with saline water sources.
  • A solar still consists of a shallow basin or container with a transparent cover or lid. The basin is typically black or dark-colored to absorb sunlight and heat up the water inside. The transparent cover allows sunlight to enter the still while trapping heat inside.
  • As sunlight passes through the transparent cover, it heats up the water in the basin. The absorbed solar energy causes the water to evaporate, forming water vapor.
  • The water vapor rises and comes into contact with the cooler surface of the transparent cover. Upon contact, the water vapor condenses back into liquid water due to the temperature difference between the vapor and the cover. The condensed water droplets collect on the underside of the cover and trickle down into a collection channel or container along the edges of the basin.
  • The condensed water collected in the channel or container is the purified distillate, free from contaminants, salts, and impurities present in the original water source.

Solar Cooker

  • A solar cooker, also known as a solar oven or solar stove, is a device that utilizes sunlight to cook food or heat water.
  • Solar cookers harness solar energy through reflective materials and insulation to create an enclosed cooking environment.
  • Solar cookers often have a reflective surface, such as mirrors or reflective panels, to concentrate sunlight onto a cooking area.
  • This reflective surface directs sunlight towards the cooking vessel, increasing the intensity of the solar energy.

Types of Solar Cookers

  1. Box cookers are insulated boxes with a transparent top and reflective panels on the sides. They are suitable for slow cooking and can reach temperatures of up to 150-200°C (300-400°F).
  1. Panel cookers use reflective panels to concentrate sunlight onto a cooking vessel placed in the center. They are lightweight, portable, and easy to set up, making them ideal for outdoor use.
  1. Parabolic cookers use a curved reflective surface to focus sunlight onto a single focal point. They can reach high temperatures quickly and are suitable for cooking a variety of foods.

Solar Pond

  • A solar pond is a large body of water with layers of increasing salt concentration, used to capture and store solar energy for various purposes such as electricity generation, heating, or desalination.
  • The concept of a solar pond relies on the principle that saltwater has different densities at different concentrations, creating a natural barrier to heat loss.
  • A solar pond is constructed with a series of layers, each containing water with varying salt concentrations. The bottom layer, known as the "hypersaline" or "convective" zone, contains highly concentrated saltwater (often saturated brine).
  • The middle layer is the "non-convective" or "gradient" zone, with a gradual increase in salt concentration.
  • The top layer, called the "surface" or "convective inhibition" zone, contains freshwater or low-salinity water.
  • Sunlight passes through the transparent surface layer and heats up the bottom layers of the pond. The highly concentrated saltwater in the bottom layer absorbs and retains solar energy, raising its temperature significantly.
  • The saltwater in the bottom layer acts as a thermal storage medium, effectively trapping solar heat within the pond. The salt gradient between the layers prevents heat from escaping through convection, creating a natural insulation barrier.
  • Heat can be extracted from the solar pond for various applications. In solar pond-based electricity generation.
  • Solar ponds can also be used for desalination by evaporating freshwater from the top layer or for direct heating of water for industrial processes, space heating, or agricultural applications.

Solar Chimney

  • A solar chimney tower, also known as a solar updraft tower or solar tower, is a type of renewable energy structure that generates electricity using solar power.
  • It harnesses the natural phenomenon of convection to create an updraft of air, which drives turbines to generate electricity.
  • The solar chimney tower consists of a tall, hollow tower with a wide collector area at its base. The tower is typically made of concrete or other durable materials and can be several hundred meters tall.
  • The collector area, located at the base of the tower, is covered with a transparent material such as glass or plastic. This material allows sunlight to pass through and heat up the air underneath.
  • Sunlight passes through the transparent collector area and heats up the air near the ground. The heated air becomes less dense and starts to rise, creating an updraft inside the tower.
  • As the hot air rises, it is drawn into the tower, creating a chimney effect. The tall height of the tower enhances this effect, creating a strong updraft of air.
  • Turbine generators are installed at the base of the tower to capture the kinetic energy of the updraft. As the hot air rises through the tower, it drives the turbines, which are connected to generators to produce electricity.
  • The rotating turbines generate electricity, which can be fed into the electrical grid for use by consumers or stored in batteries for later use.

Solar Photovoltaic Systems

Solar Cell Fundamentals

Photovoltaic Effect:

  • At the heart of a solar cell is the photovoltaic effect, which is the process by which certain materials generate an electric current when exposed to light.
  • This effect was first observed by French physicist Alexandre-Edmond Becquerel in 1839.

Semiconductor Materials:

  • Solar cells are typically made from semiconductor materials such as silicon.
    • Semiconductors have properties that allow them to conduct electricity under certain conditions.
    • Silicon is the most commonly used semiconductor material in solar cells due to its abundance and favorable electrical properties.

P-N Junction:

  • A basic solar cell consists of two layers of semiconductor material - a P-type layer and an N-type layer - sandwiched together to form a P-N junction.
  • When light strikes the solar cell, it excites electrons in the semiconductor material, creating electron-hole pairs.
  • The electric field at the P-N junction then separates these charges, generating a voltage difference and thus creating an electric current.

Band Gap:

  • The band gap is a crucial property of semiconductor materials. It represents the energy difference between the valence band (where electrons are tightly bound) and the conduction band (where electrons are free to move).
  • Photons from sunlight must have energy greater than the band gap of the semiconductor material to generate electron-hole pairs efficiently.

Energy Conversion:

  • When sunlight (photons) hits the solar cell, it transfers its energy to electrons in the semiconductor material, causing them to move and creating an electric current.
  • This current flows through an external circuit, providing electrical power.

Cell Efficiency:

  • The efficiency of a solar cell refers to the percentage of sunlight that it converts into electricity.
  • Improving efficiency is a significant focus of research and development in the solar industry, as higher efficiency means more electricity can be generated from the same amount of sunlight.

Module and Array:

  • Solar cells are typically interconnected to form larger units called solar modules or panels. These modules can be further combined into arrays to meet specific power requirements.
    • The electrical output of solar modules and arrays depends on factors such as the number of cells, their efficiency, and environmental conditions.
    • Understanding these fundamentals helps in designing and optimizing solar cells and systems for maximum efficiency and performance.
    • Ongoing research aims to improve solar cell efficiency, reduce costs, and expand the range of materials and technologies available for solar energy generation.

Characteristics of a PV Cell

  1. Material: Photovoltaic cells are typically made of semiconductor materials, most commonly crystalline silicon. Other materials used include amorphous silicon, cadmium telluride (CdTe), copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS), and organic photovoltaic materials.
  1. Energy Conversion: Photovoltaic cells convert sunlight directly into electricity through the photovoltaic effect. When sunlight (photons) strikes the cell, it excites electrons in the semiconductor material, creating an electric current. This direct conversion of light into electricity is a hallmark characteristic of PV cells.
  1. Efficiency: The efficiency of a PV cell refers to its ability to convert sunlight into electricity. Efficiency is typically expressed as a percentage and represents the ratio of the electrical power output of the cell to the solar power input (sunlight) under standard test conditions. PV cell efficiencies can vary widely depending on factors such as the materials used, manufacturing processes, and cell design.
  1. Voltage and Current: Photovoltaic cells generate a direct current (DC) output when exposed to sunlight. The voltage and current produced by the cell depend on factors such as the intensity of sunlight, cell temperature, and load connected to the cell. The voltage-current (IV) curve of a PV cell describes its electrical characteristics under different operating conditions.
  1. Temperature Dependence: The performance of photovoltaic cells is affected by temperature, with higher temperatures typically resulting in reduced efficiency. As the temperature of the cell increases, the voltage decreases while the current may increase slightly. This phenomenon is known as the temperature coefficient of the PV cell.
  1. Spectral Response: PV cells have different responses to sunlight depending on the wavelength or color of the light. The spectral response curve describes how efficiently the cell converts light at different wavelengths into electricity. Most PV cells are more sensitive to shorter wavelengths in the visible spectrum, with reduced sensitivity to longer wavelengths such as infrared.
  1. Durability and Reliability: Photovoltaic cells are designed to withstand various environmental conditions, including sunlight exposure, temperature fluctuations, humidity, and mechanical stress. Durability and reliability are essential characteristics to ensure the long-term performance and lifespan of PV modules.

Classification of Solar PV Cells

Solar photovoltaic (PV) cells can be classified based on various criteria, including the materials used, cell structure, and manufacturing techniques.

Based on Materials Used

  1. Crystalline Silicon Cells

    These are the most widely used PV cells and are made of crystalline silicon wafers.

    They are further classified into:

    1. Monocrystalline Silicon Cells: Made from a single crystal structure, typically exhibiting higher efficiency and higher cost.
    1. Polycrystalline Silicon Cells: Made from multiple silicon crystals, usually less expensive but with slightly lower efficiency compared to monocrystalline cells.
  1. Thin-Film Cells

    These cells use thin layers of semiconductor materials deposited on substrates.

    Common thin-film materials include amorphous silicon (a-Si), cadmium telluride (CdTe), and copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS).

    Thin-film cells are typically less expensive to produce but may have lower efficiency compared to crystalline silicon cells.

Based on Cell Structure

  1. Single-Junction Cells: These cells consist of a single semiconductor material and are the most common type of PV cell.
  1. Multijunction Cells: These cells incorporate multiple semiconductor layers with different bandgaps to capture a broader spectrum of sunlight, thus achieving higher efficiency. Multijunction cells are often used in concentrated photovoltaic (CPV) systems.
  1. Heterojunction Cells: These cells use different semiconductor materials at the junction interface, such as amorphous silicon and crystalline silicon, to improve efficiency and performance.
  1. Perovskite Cells: Perovskite solar cells are a promising emerging technology that utilizes perovskite materials as the light-absorbing layer. They offer the potential for high efficiency and low-cost manufacturing.

Based on Manufacturing Technique

  1. Wafer-Based Cells: These cells are manufactured using silicon wafers sliced from ingots produced through the Czochralskior Float Zone method.
  1. Thin-Film Cells: These cells are produced using deposition techniques such as chemical vapor deposition (CVD), physical vapor deposition (PVD), or solution-based methods to deposit thin layers of semiconductor materials onto substrates.
  1. Organic Photovoltaic Cells: These cells use organic (carbon-based) materials as the active layer, offering the potential for flexible, lightweight, and low-cost solar cells.

Based on Application

  1. Residential PV Cells: These are designed for rooftop solar installations on residential buildings, typically focusing on a balance between efficiency and cost-effectiveness.
  1. Commercial/Utility-Scale PV Cells: These cells are optimized for large-scale solar power plants, prioritizing high efficiency and reliability to maximize energy production and return on investment.

Based on Efficiency Class

PV cells can be classified based on their efficiency class, ranging from standard efficiency to high efficiency and beyond.

Higher efficiency cells typically command a higher price but offer greater energy production for a given area.

Array Construction

  • Array involves arranging multiple PV panels in a specific configuration to efficiently capture sunlight and convert it into electricity.
  • Before beginning construction, conduct a thorough site assessment to determine factors such as available space, solar irradiance, shading, and orientation. This information will help optimize the layout and positioning of the PV array for maximum energy production.
  • Based on the site assessment, design the layout of the PV array. Consider factors such as the tilt angle, azimuth orientation, spacing between panels, and any shading obstacles. The design should aim to maximize solar exposure throughout the day and across seasons.
  • Install the mounting system for the PV array. This can include ground-mounted racks, roof-mounted structures, or other mounting solutions depending on the site characteristics and project requirements. Ensure that the mounting system is securely anchored and properly aligned according to the design specifications.

Panel Construction

  • Constructing a photovoltaic (PV) panel involves assembling photovoltaic cells into a larger unit capable of capturing sunlight and converting it into electricity.
  • PV cells come in various types, such as monocrystalline, polycrystalline, and thin-film.
  • Once the cells are selected, they are interconnected using conductive materials such as soldering ribbons or busbars to create an electrical circuit for the generated electricity to flow.
  • The interconnected cells are typically encapsulated within a protective material, such as tempered glass or a durable polymer, to protect them from environmental factors like moisture and physical damage.
  • The encapsulated cells are mounted onto a sturdy frame, often made of aluminum or another lightweight, corrosion-resistant material, which provides structural support and facilitates the installation of the PV panel.
  • A junction box is attached to the PV panel, providing secure housing for electrical connections. This box also typically includes diodes to prevent reverse current flow and protect the panel from potential damage.
  • Rigorous testing is conducted to ensure the PV panel meets performance standards and safety regulations, including electrical testing, insulation resistance testing, and visual inspection.
